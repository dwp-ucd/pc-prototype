How much is the State Pension?

How much State Pension you get depends on your National Insurance record.

The full basic State Pension (if you reached State Pension age before 6 April 2016) is £141.85 per week.

The full new State Pension (if the date you reach State Pension age is on or after 6 April 2016) is £185.15 per week.

You can get more State Pension if:

  • you are eligible for Additional State Pension
  • you delay (defer) taking your State Pensio

You can still get a State Pension if you have other income like a personal pension or a workplace pension.

You may have to pay tax on your State Pension.

If you have not yet reached State Pension age, check your State Pension forecast to find out how much you could get and when.

Qualifying for the full amount

You usually need a total of: 

  • 30 qualifying years of National Insurance contributions or credits to get the full basic State Pension 
  • 35 qualifying years to get the full new State Pension

These qualifying years do not have to be in a row. There might be gaps. Check your National Insurance record to find out how many qualifying years you have.

National Insurance qualifying years are years in which you have:

  • worked and paid National Insurance
  • got National Insurance Credits, for example you were unemployed, sick, or a parent or carer
  • paid voluntary National Insurance contributions
If you get the new State Pension (the date you reach State Pension age is on or after 6 April 2016) and some of your qualifying years are from before 6 April 2016, you may get more or less than the full amount of State Pension.

If you do not qualify for a full State Pension you might be able to increase the amount you get.

Your spouse or civil partner’s pension

Your State Pension is usually based on your own National Insurance record. But, in some cases you might inherit State Pension or increase it through a spouse or civil partner.

Annual increases

The State Pension increases every year by whichever is the highest of the following:

  • earnings - the average percentage growth in wages (in Great Britain)
  • prices - the percentage growth in prices in the UK as measured by the Consumer Prices Index (CPI)
  • 2.5%

If you have a protected payment, it increases each year in line with the CPI.

Temporary change for the tax year starting April 2022

The State Pension has increased by 3.1% this year, in line with CPI.

The average earnings growth was higher, but those figures were affected by the coronavirus (COVID-19) pandemic and so they were not taken into account.

If you think your State Pension amount is wrong

Contact the pensions service if you’re not sure your State Pension amount is correct.

Adult Dependency Increase and other help you could get

Adult Dependency Increase payments have stopped. You may be eligible to apply for Pension Credit or Universal Credit.
